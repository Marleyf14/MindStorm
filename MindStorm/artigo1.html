The Dance of the Sun: May
2024 Solar Storms




The Sun releases charged particles (protons and electrons) in events such as solar flares and CMEs. When these particles are ejected toward
Earth, they form the solar wind, a constant stream of charged particles that travels through space.

When the solar wind reaches Earth, it collides with the magnetosphere, the protective layer formed by our planetâ€™s magnetic field. During
intense events, such as a CME, the impact is strong enough to compress the magnetosphere.

Impacts of Solar Storms in May
2024

In May 2024, Earth experienced one of the
most intense geomagnetic storms in more
than 20 years. Between May 3 and 9, a series
of solar flares and coronal mass ejections
(CMEs) from active regions on the Sun
created significant disturbances. These solar
events launched clouds of charged particles
toward Earth, leading to a geomagnetic storm
classified as G5, the highest level on the
scale, that has not been seen since 2003.

The most powerful solar flare recorded during
this period was on May 14, classified as an X8.7
flare, the largest of current Solar Cycle 25. This
storm produced stunning auroras visible in
regions as far south as Texas and Alabama,
creating vibrant displays of light around the
world. Reports of auroras from citizen scientists
have contributed valuable data, enhancing our
understanding of these phenomena.

The storm also raised concerns for power grids,
satellites and space missions, prompting
preventive measures to mitigate its effects.
NASA and other agencies are using data from
this event to improve their models and better
predict future solar storm impacts.
